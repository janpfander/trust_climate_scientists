Behavioral measures

Donation

To assess consequential and impactful behavior, participants will be given the option to donate a monetary bonus to the American Geophysical Union (AGU), or keep it for themselves. The AGU was chosen because it is the leading scientific association for scientists working on climate change. Participants read following:

“For the next question, we would like you to have the opportunity to donate money to a non-profit or to keep it for yourself. Please decide how you would like to allocate $10 between yourself and the American Geophysical Union (AGU). The AGU is a non-profit, non-partisan scientific association. It is the largest scientific association in the area of climate change in the US, with over 15,000 scientist members. You can give all of the money to the AGU and keep none for yourself, or you can keep all the money for yourself and give none to AGU, or you can pick any split in between. Note: We will randomly select a subset of 100 participants and realize their choices.”

Scientists’ role in policy making

Scale: from 0 to 100 (sliders)

Scales were adapted from the “Normative perceptions of science and society” scale in Cologna et al. (2025), but slightly modified: We removed two items, one because it was not strictly true (“Climate scientists should remain independent from the policy-making process”) and the other because it was not policy related (“Climate scientists should communicate their findings to the general public”). We also use sliders instead of a 5-point likert scale.

Item Response Options
To what extent do you agree or disagree with the following statements?
Climate scientists should work closely with politicians to integrate scientific results into policy-making 0 = Strongly disagree … 100 = Strongly agree
Climate scientists should actively advocate for specific policies 0 = Strongly disagree … 100 = Strongly agree
Climate scientists should communicate their findings to politicians 0 = Strongly disagree … 100 = Strongly agree
Climate scientists should be more involved in the policy-making process 0 = Strongly disagree … 100 = Strongly agree

Reliance on scientists for lifestyle choices

Scale: from 0 to 100 (sliders)

Adapted from the “Willingness to be vulnerable to scientists” scale in Cologna et al. (2025), but heavily modified. We retain only one item, as the other two (“How much or little should governments rely on climate scientists’ guidance when making decisions related to climate science?” and “How much or little control do you want climate scientists to have on government decisions related to science?”) were conceptually closer to scientists’ role in policy making. We also make changes to the one item we retain (original: “How willing are you to rely on climate scientists’ advice when making lifestyle choices (e.g., sustainable transportation, energy use and nutrition)? (1) Not at all, (2) A little, (3) A moderate amount, (4) A lot, (5) A great deal”), as the endpoints of the original likert scale might be asymmetric. Our modification also aligns better with our scale for measuring scientists’ role in policy making.

Item Response Options
To what extent do you agree or disagree with the following statement?
When making lifestyle choices (e.g., sustainable transportation, energy use and nutrition), I am willing to rely on climate scientists’ advice. 0 = not at all … 100 = Strongly agree

Single item trust in climate scientists

We add a single item question on trust in climate scientists, to compare how this maps onto the different dimensions of our multi-dimension main outcome measure:

“How much do you trust climate scientists?” (slider: 0 = not at all, 100 = very strongly)

Single item distrust in climate scientists

Adding an item on distrust and comparing it to the trust item will allow us to test if they are symmetrical or not.

“How much do you distrust climate scientists?” (slider: 0 = not at all, 100 = very strongly)

Support for climate policies

Adapted from Cologna et al. (2025), who used a modified version from Vlasceanu et al. (2024). We use sliders instead of the original 3-point scale.

Item Response Options
How much do you support the following policies.
Raising carbon taxes on fossil fuels (e.g., gas, oil, coal) 0 = not at all … 100 = Very strongly
Expanding infrastructure for public transportation 0 = not at all … 100 = Very strongly
Increasing the use of sustainable energy such as wind and solar energy 0 = not at all … 100 = Very strongly
Protecting forested and land areas 0 = not at all … 100 = Very strongly
Increasing taxes on carbon intense foods (e.g., beef and dairy products) 0 = not at all … 100 = Very strongly

We could also use the more extensive scale from Vlasceanu et al. (2024):

Climate change policy support. This dependent variable consisted of participants’ level of agreement from 0 = Not at all to 100 = Very much so using a slider (participants could also respond with “not applicable”, which is coded as “NA” in the dataset), with the following nine statements: “I support raising carbon taxes on gas/fossil fuels/coal,” “I support significantly expanding infrastructure for public transportation,” “I support increasing the number of charging stations for electric vehicles,” “I support increasing the use of sustainable energy such as wind and solar energy,” “I support increasing taxes on airline companies to offset carbon emissions,” “I support protecting forested and land areas,” “I support investing more in green jobs and businesses,” “I support introducing laws to keep waterways and oceans clean,” and “I support increasing taxes on carbon-intensive foods (for example, meat and dairy).”

Funding for research on climate change

Adapted from the National Science Foundation (NSF) – Science & Engineering Indicators, also used in Ophir et al. (2023).

“Climate change research that advances the frontiers of knowledge is necessary and should be supported by funding from the federal government.” (slider, 0 = Strongly disagree, 100 = Strongly agree]

Belief in climate change

From Vlasceanu et al. (2024).

How accurate do you think this statement is? “Human activities are causing climate change.” (slider, 0 = not at all accurate, 100 = extremely accurate)

Belief in climate crisis

From Vlasceanu et al. (2024).

“How accurate do you think these statements are?” (slider, 0 = not at all accurate, 100 = extremely accurate)

  • “Taking action to fight climate change is necessary to avoid a global catastrophe,”
  • “Climate change poses a serious threat to humanity,”
  • “Climate change is a global emergency.”
