1. Trust in science matters for mitigating climate change

Mitigating climate change is urgent (Rogelj et al. 2023; Calvin et al. 2023)

People who trust scientists (in general) more, also tend to…

… be more likely to accept the scientific consensus on global warming (Bogert et al. 2024)

… have more accurate beliefs about climate change (Ejaz, Vu, and Fletcher 2025)

… support climate policies (Cologna and Siegrist 2020; Hornsey et al. 2016)

A large study across 55 countries found that trust in climate scientists more specifically was the strongest predictor of belief in climate change and support for climate policies (Todorova et al. 2025)

Add a footnote here among how many variables, what kind of variables, and two examples

But…

all of this evidence is correlational.

How to strenghten trust in scientists?

  • using open-science practices (Rosman et al. 2022; Song, Markowitz, and Taylor 2022)
  • highlighting successful replications (Hendriks, Kienhues, and Bromme 2020)
  • communicating uncertainty (Schneider et al. 2022)
  • presenting participants with an infographic about the scientific process (Agley et al. 2021)
  • have scientists use two-sided rather than one-sided arguments (Hendriks, Janssen, and Jucks 2023)
  • presenting participanats with short biographies of scientists (Hautea, Besley, and Choung 2024)

Few studies on climate scientists more specifically, but see (Palm, Bolsen, and Kingsland 2020; Orchinik et al. 2024; Koetke et al. 2024)

  • after receiving a message about the need for changes in individual behavior or in public policy to address climate change, people tend to trust climate scientists less (Palm, Bolsen, and Kingsland 2020).

  • participants perceived climate scientists as more skilled—a dimension of trustworthiness—after reading a text about the longstanding history and foundations of climate science (Orchinik et al. 2024).

  • Exercising intellectual humility—acknowledging the limits of one’s knowledge—increased trust in a fictive climate scientists (Koetke et al. 2024).

But…


There are also null results

e.g., in the US, messages tailored to conservatives did not change their trust in scientists (Gligorić, Van Kleef, and Rutjens 2025)


And mixed findings

e.g., effects of communicating scientific uncertainties are highly context-dependent, and some studies find negative effects (Schuster and Scheu 2026; Bles et al. 2020)

In summary…

  • relatively few studies

  • even fewer on climate scientists specifically

  • results are hard to compare due to different research designs

What is a megastudy and why do one?

A megastudy is…

“a massive field experiment in which many different treatments are tested synchronously in one large sample using a common, objectively measured outcome” (Milkman et al. 2021)


Often takes the form of an “intervention turnament”

  • a public call for intervention submissions
  • most promising submissions get tested
  • the best intervention wins

It’s like the perfect meta-analysis.

A risk with meta-analyses is always to compare apples and oranges.

Existing behavioral megastudies have targeted, e.g.,

  • physical exercise (Milkman et al. 2021)
  • vaccination (Milkman et al. 2024; Milkman et al. 2022)
  • partisan animosity (Voelkel, Stagnaro, et al. 2024)
  • tax report honesty (Zickfeld et al. 2024)
  • depression (Kaveladze et al. 2026)
  • climate advocacy (Goldwert et al. 2026)
  • climate action (Sinclair et al. 2025; Voelkel et al. 2026)
  • support for carbon pricing (Huber et al. 2026)

The not always explicity claim to be a megastudy, but have some recurrent characteristics.

  • big sample
  • many intervention arms
  • often the result of a crowdsourced collaboration tournament

There are even several guides to megastudies (Duckworth and Milkman 2022; Doell 2023; Voelkel, Chu, et al. 2024)

Outcomes

Primary outcome: Trust in climate scientists

A multidimensional 12-item measure (based on Cologna et al. 2025)

(scale from 0 (low trust) to 100 (high trust))

  • competence (e.g., “How unqualified or qualified are most climate scientists?”)
  • benevolence (e.g., “How uneager or eager are most climate scientists to improve others’ lives?”)
  • integrity (e.g., “How dishonest or honest are most climate scientists?”)
  • openness (e.g., “How unwilling or willing are most climate scientists to be transparent?”)

To ensure that all interventions would focus on this target, we did not communicate secondary or tertiary outcomes to research teams during the call for intervention submissions (see below).

Secondary outcomes

Including, e.g.,

  • role of scientists in policy making (e.g., “Climate scientists should actively advocate for specific policies.” - disagree/agree)
  • donation to the American Meteorological Society (AMS) (lottery, between $0 and $10)
  • signup to climate scientists Katherine Hayhoe’s newsletter (Yes/No self-report + click tracking)

Secondary and tertiary outcomes capture downstream attitudinal and behavioral consequences of trust, as well as related constructs.

Tertiary outcomes

Including, e.g.,

  • belief in climate change (e.g., “Human activities are causing climate change” - not accurate/accurate)
  • individual behaviors (e.g., “Go on less personal air travel” - not likely/likely)
  • policy preferences (e.g., “Raising taxes on fossil fuels” - oppose/support)

The distinction between secondary and tertiary outcomes reflects theoretical proximity to the primary outcome — secondary outcomes such as institutional trust and funding perceptions are more directly related to trust in climate scientists, while tertiary outcomes such as behavioral intentions and policy support are more distal. Practical relevance also plays a role: behavioral measures and funding support are designated secondary due to their direct policy implications. We distinguish between outcome groups primarily to contextualize findings in light of multiple comparison concerns.

Selection

  • Each of the 105 submission was independently reviewed by three randomly assigned reviewers from the advisory board

  • The reviewers had fixed evaluation criteria, but they could subjectively weight them

  • Because reviewers rated very differently, we standardized scores within reviewers

  • Final score: the average of the weighted standardized scores

  • We picked the 20 highest scoring interventions

Figure 2: Distribtions of weights assigned by reviewers. Each dot corresponds to one reviewer’s weight for the respective rating dimension.

Reviewers provided ratings on four dimensions:

1. Theoretical grounding Is the intervention based on sound and convincing theory?

2. Theoretical insight Would testing this intervention advance theoretical understanding? (Interventions with clear, single mechanisms may offer stronger insight than those combining many mechanisms.)

3. Odds of success How plausible is it that the intervention will work in our study? Helpful questions include: - Has it been tested before? - Was the context comparable (U.S. sample, setting, outcomes)? - How large were the effect sizes?

4. Practical relevance How relevant and scalable is the intervention in the real world? (E.g., can it be implemented easily? Is it feasible at scale?)

Figure 3: Distribtions of reviewer scores (weighted averages of the different rating categories). Each dot corresponds to a review. Lines with dot in the middle (above the boxplots) represent the reviewer specific means and their 95% CIs.

Methodological challenges

Multiple comparison

  • Some studies account for it (Milkman et al. 2022), some don’t (e.g., Voelkel, Stagnaro, et al. 2024)
  • You can argue it depends on what you consider a “family of tests”
  • In any case:
    • if you do null hypothesis significance testing, testing many effects makes it more likely to obtain statistically significant results by chance alone
  • so, you need some kind of strategy, or you end up doing data dredging

Multiple comparison

Statistical accounting for multiple comparison can allevate this issue.

However, even if you do it, there are many ways to do this…

  • adjust across only interventions
  • adjust between interventions + outcomes
  • adjust between moderator levels

Many degrees of freedom -> specific preregistering is crucial.

We haven’t simulated how different adjustment measures would affect statistical power.

Besides statistical corrections, we tried to account for this issue with the primary, secondary, tertiary outcome hierarchy in addition.

Differential attrition

  • Happens when, after treatment assignment, attrition differs systematically between experimental conditions
  • The more experimental conditions, and the more different they are, the more likely
  • Clearly, megastudies are very vulnerable to differential attriton

Differential attrition

Example:

  • Some people are pissed off by interventions trying to convince them of climate scientists’ trustworthiness
  • These people are likely to have relatively low baseline trust
  • Some interventions may try harder to convince than others (e.g., by being longer)

Figure 14: Per participant: hybrid intervention_time (direct timer sum for 15 fully-timed conditions; complement minus duration-proportional imputed correction for 6 multi-page conditions). See data/cleaning.qmd. Conditions ordered by median.

Differential attrition

If low-trust people drop out after longer treatments more than after shorter ones, then…

… long treatment conditions loose a bunch of low trust people (compared to all other conditions)

… their treatment effects get overestimated

Should we be doing only megastudies?

+

  • high powered, high quality, directly comparable test of many interventions
  • collaborative science and inclusion of early career researchers
  • practical relevance

Megastudies can be the best version of a meta-analysis

-

  • megastudies provide litte insight into how exactly interventions work
  • while they are somewhat inclusive, they also create inequalities
  • require massive budgets, so effectively, only researchers in rich institutions can lead them
- not so clear how much being a collaborator will contribute to early researchers' career
  • hot take: megastudies might not always be as practically relevant as they are sold

Psychological experiments usually have several conditions that allow to make sepcific claims about how and under which circumstances a manipulation works

Megastudies focus on individuals

  • in an i-frame vs. s-frame debaste (Chater and Loewenstein 2023), megastudies are clearly i-frame
  • yet, for trust in scientists specifically, we know that structural factors like representation matter a lot (Druckman et al. 2025)

Outlook and conclusion

  • Data collection to be completed within the next ~5 weeks
    • including a follow-up survey to test persistence after ~1 week
  • What we expect:
    • small effects
    • which might not persist
  • What we do not expect:
    • solve deep-seeted structural issues that may foster distrust/lack of trust in science among some groups

So why this study then?

  • small effects may scale
  • scientists, policy makers, NGOs, journalists need to communicate about climate science — we might as well help them use effective strategies
  • our research group plans to translate interventions into real-life with stakeholders
